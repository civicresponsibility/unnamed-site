Our Theory of Change

Decades of research has found that money has little effect on how a particular member of Congress will vote on the floor (see Stratmann 2005). Accordingly, we are not trying to bribe Congress. But money affects who can afford to run for Congress, who gets elected, and who has access to legislators once they take office (see Powell & Grimmer 2014).

Campaign finance reform is a long-term strategy. In the meanwhile, we hope to raise the tide of small-donor supporters and shift the balance of power away from concentrated wealth.

Here’s why we think this works:

Targeting real outcomes

Campaign contributions made here on if.then.fund are based on what legislators do, not what they say they will do.

After a vote, we’ll tell members of Congress why you made your contribution.

Shaping the next Congress

Keep ’em in or throw ’em out. Donations can be made to incumbents who support your position or to their next general election challenger if they vote the other way.

Reaching all members of Congress

Members of Congress may only be accountable to their constituents, but committee chairs and party leaders make policy for us all. if.then.fund lets you shape the whole Congress.
